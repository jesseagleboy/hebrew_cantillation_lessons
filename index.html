Shva rules — שְׁוָא
Shva Na שְׁוָא נָע
Moving shva — pronounced like a very short "e." Occurs at the start of a word, under the first of two consecutive shva-bearing letters, after a long vowel, or under a letter with dagesh chazak.
e.g. בְּרֵאשִׁית — the first בְּ is shva na (start of word)
First of two consecutive shvasשְׁמַרְתֶּם — the שְׁ is na; the מְ that follows is nach
After a long vowelקָרְאוּ — the רְ follows a kamatz (long), so it is na
Under dagesh chazakהַמְּלָכִים — the מְּ bears a dagesh chazak, making its shva na
Shva Nach שְׁוָא נָח
Resting shva — silent. Closes a syllable. Occurs at the end of a syllable, after a short vowel, or under the second of two consecutive shva letters.
e.g. מַלְכּוּת — the לְ is shva nach, closing the first syllable
Second of two consecutive shvasיִשְׁמְרוּ — the first שְׁ is na, the second מְ is nach
After a short vowelכָּתַבְתָּ — the בְ follows a short patach → nach, closing the syllable
End of wordנֶפֶשׁ — the שׁ at the end of the word has a nach (always silent at word's end)
Shva at end
A shva at the very end of a word is always shva nach — never pronounced.
e.g. אֶרֶץ — final tsadi has a silent shva nach
יֶלֶד — the final דְ is silent nach
מֶלֶךְ — the final כְ is silent nach
נֶפֶשׁ — the final שׁ is silent nach
Chataf vowels חֲטָפִים
Guttural letters (א ה ח ע) cannot take a silent shva, so they take a chataf — a "half vowel." Chataf patach (◌ֲ), chataf segol (◌ֱ), chataf kamatz (◌ֳ) are always pronounced.
e.g. אֱלֹקִים — chataf segol (◌ֱ) under aleph
Chataf patach (ֲ)חֲלוֹם — under chet; most common chataf
Chataf segol (ֱ)אֱלֹקִים — under aleph; very common
Chataf kamatz (ֳ) — rareעֳנִי — under ayin; found only a handful of times in the Torah
Dagesh rules — דָּגֵשׁ
Dagesh Kal דָּגֵשׁ קַל
Appears only in the six BeGeD KeFaT letters (בגדכפת) at the start of a word, after a shva nach, or after a closed syllable. Changes the pronunciation from a fricative to a stop.
בּ = b (not v), כּ = k (not ch), פּ = p (not f)
Start of wordבְּרֵאשִׁית — בּ is a stop (b); without dagesh it would be v
After shva nachיִפְתַּח — the פּ follows a shva nach → dagesh kal → p not f
After closed syllableמִכְתָּב — כּ and תּ both take dagesh kal after the closed syllables that precede them
Dagesh Chazak דָּגֵשׁ חָזָק
Doubles the letter. Can appear in any letter except gutturals (אהחע) and ר. The vowel before a dagesh chazak is always short. Indicates a shva na if under the doubled letter.
e.g. הַמֶּלֶךְ — the מּ is doubled
Definite article + ה assimilationהַנָּהָר — the נּ doubles because the ה of הַ assimilates into it
Verbal doubling (Pi'el)דִּבֶּר — the בּ is doubled; pi'el binyan always doubles the middle root letter
Assimilated נהִגִּיד — the גּ is doubled because an original נ assimilated into it (root נ.ג.ד)
Mappiq מַפִּיק
A dot in a final ה (הּ) — makes it a pronounced consonant, not a vowel letter. Rare; signals a possessive suffix.
e.g. גְּבוּלָהּ — "her border"
בְּיָדָהּ — "in her hand" (Bereishit 38:18)
רוּחָהּ — "her spirit/breath"; the הּ is fully pronounced
נַפְשָׁהּ — "her soul"; contrast with נַפְשָׁה (no mappiq) = "her soul" as a vowel letter — context tells you which
Gutturals — no dagesh
The letters א ה ח ע (and usually ר) never take a dagesh of any kind. When a dagesh chazak would be expected, the preceding vowel is often lengthened instead (compensatory lengthening).
e.g. יְבָרֶכְךָ — the ר cannot double
Compensatory lengtheningמֵאַרְצְךָ — the א cannot take dagesh, so the preceding vowel lengthens to tsere
Resh — treated as gutturalבֵּרַךְ — the ר cannot take dagesh even though the root (ב.ר.כ) in pi'el would expect it
Virtual doublingSometimes the vowel before a guttural simply stays short without lengthening — called "virtual doubling" (the doubling is implied but not marked)
Stress rules — טַעַם
Mil'ra מִלְרַע
Stress on the last syllable — the most common pattern in Biblical Hebrew.
e.g. דָּבָר — stress on בָר (last syllable)
אֱלֹקִים — stress on הִים (last syllable)
שָׁמַיִם — stress on יִם
כָּתַב — stress on טַב (qal perfect 3ms — mil'ra)
Mil'el מִלְעֵיל
Stress on the second-to-last (penultimate) syllable. Common in certain verb forms (like the imperative and some perfects), with certain suffixes, or when indicated by the trop.
e.g. אֶ֫רֶץ — stress on אֶ, not ץ
Imperativeשְׁמַ֫ע — "hear!" — stress on שְׁמַ (imperative often mil'el)
Qal perfect 1csכָּתַ֫בְתִּי — stress retreats to כָּתַ before the תִּי suffix
Segolate nounsמֶ֫לֶךְ, סֵ֫פֶר, קֹ֫דֶשׁ — the whole class of segolate nouns is mil'el
Nasog Achor נָסוֹג אָחוֹר
"Retreating accent" — stress shifts back to avoid two consecutive stressed syllables between adjacent words. Very common in leining.
e.g. בֹּ֫קֶר טוֹב — stress retreats on בֹּקֶר
טוֹב מְאֹד — "very good" (Bereishit 1:31) — stress on טוֹב retreats away from מְאֹד
כִּי־טוֹב — when joined by maqqef, accent retreats on כִּי to avoid clash with טוֹב
אֶחָ֫ד הָיָה — "there was one" — the הָיָה following causes retreat on אֶחָד
Meteg מֶתֶג
A small vertical line to the left of a vowel — marks a secondary stress or indicates a long vowel that must not be reduced, especially before shva.
e.g. וְהָֽאָרֶץ — meteg under the kamatz, keeping it full "ah"
Prevents reduction before shvaבָּֽרָא — the kamatz under בָּ has meteg; without it, it might reduce to a shva
Secondary stress in long wordsוַיְהִֽי־כֵן — marks a secondary rhythmic beat in the phrase
Before composite shvaיָֽדַע — the kamatz before the composite shva must be held long; meteg signals this
Ta'amei hamikra — hierarchy of disjunctives
Trop marks serve two functions: musical (melody) and syntactic (punctuation). Disjunctives separate clauses; conjunctives connect a word to the next disjunctive. Higher-rank disjunctives = stronger pauses = bigger syntactic breaks.
Rank Name Hebrew Function
1 — Emperor Sof pasuk סוֹף פָּסוּק ׃ End of verse. Full stop. Always present.
1 — Emperor Etnachta אֶתְנַחְתָּא ֑ Major mid-verse pause. Divides the verse into two halves. Like a semicolon.
2 — King Segol סֶגּוֹל ֒ Strong pause in the first half of the verse (before etnachta).
2 — King Shalshelet שַׁלְשֶׁלֶת ֓ Rare, drawn-out trop. Long melodic chain. Strong disjunctive with dramatic effect.
2 — King Zakef katon זָקֵף קָטוֹן ֔ Common strong pause. Subdivides sections within a half-verse.
2 — King Zakef gadol זָקֵף גָּדוֹל ֕ Stronger version of zakef katon. Less common.
3 — Duke Tifcha טִפְחָא ֖ Very common. Precedes (and serves) sof pasuk or etnachta. Like a comma.
3 — Duke Revia רְבִיעַ ֗ Moderate disjunctive. Subdivides zakef territory.
3 — Duke Zarka זַרְקָא ֘ Precedes segol. Paired disjunctive.
3 — Duke Pashta פַּשְׁטָא ֙ Common disjunctive in the second half of a verse. Appears after the word's main vowel.
3 — Duke Yetiv יְתִיב ֚ Weak disjunctive; precedes tifcha. Appears on monosyllabic words or mil'el words.
3 — Duke Tevir תְּבִיר ֛ Common in second half. Subordinate to tifcha and etnachta.
4 — Count Geresh גֵּרֵשׁ ֜ Lighter disjunctive pause. Subdivides within duke territory.
4 — Count Gershayim גֵּרְשַׁיִם ֞ Stronger than geresh. Two marks; used when geresh would be ambiguous.
4 — Count Pazer פָּזֵר ֡ Emphatic disjunctive. Marks lists or special semantic emphasis.
4 — Count Telisha gedola תְּלִישָׁא גְּדוֹלָה ֠ Appears at the beginning of a word. Weaker disjunctive in longer verses.
Conjunctive Munach מוּנַח ֣ Most common conjunctive. Connects word to the following disjunctive.
Conjunctive Mercha מֵרְכָא ֥ Second most common conjunctive. Often precedes tifcha or sof pasuk.
Conjunctive Darga דַּרְגָּא ֧ Conjunctive serving tevir.
Conjunctive Kadma קַדְמָא ֨ Conjunctive for pashta and geresh.
Conjunctive Azla (Geresh) אַזְלָא ֜ Same shape as geresh; context determines if disjunctive or conjunctive.
Key dikduk rules for leining
Kamatz vs Kamatz Katan
A regular kamatz (ָ) is the long "ah" vowel. A kamatz katan looks identical but is pronounced as a short "o" (like cholam). It occurs in a closed unaccented syllable — context and trop position determine which it is.
e.g. כָּל = "kol" — kamatz katan (closed, unaccented syllable)
Kamatz katanחָכְמָה = "chokh-mah" — first syllable is closed and unaccented → "o"
Kamatz katanעָמְדוּ = "om-du" — standing; first syllable closed → "o"
Regular kamatz (gadol)אָמַר = "a-mar" — open, accented → long "ah"; never "o"
Dalet vs Resh
ד with a dagesh = "d." Without dagesh, Ashkenazim pronounce it as "d" anyway; Sephardim use a softer sound. ר never takes a dagesh — if you see what looks like a dot in a resh, it's part of the vowel system, not a dagesh.
e.g. דָּבָר — the first דּ has dagesh kal (d); the ר never has a dagesh
אֲדֹנָי — the דּ has dagesh kal after the chataf; the ר in אָרֶץ never does
Common mistakeבָּרָא — both ר letters have no dagesh; what looks like a dot in some fonts is a vowel mark, not a dagesh
Pi'el with reshבֵּרַךְ — in pi'el, the middle root letter is doubled; but ר resists → no dagesh, vowel may compensate
Patach Genuvah פַּתַח גְּנוּבָה
"Stolen patach" — a patach appearing under a final guttural (ח, ע, or הּ) that is pronounced before the consonant, not after it.
e.g. רוּחַ — pronounced "ru-ach" not "ru-cha"
שָׁמֵעַ — "sha-me-a" — the patach is stolen before the ayin
גָּבֹהַּ — "ga-vo-ah" — patach before the final הּ (mappiq)
Leining tipIf you see a patach under a final ח or ע, always pronounce the vowel before the consonant — never after
Kri / Ktiv קְרִי / כְּתִיב
What's written (ktiv) vs. what's read (kri). The vowels in the text belong to the kri word, not the ktiv word. Most famous: the four-letter name of ה׳ — the ktiv letters carry the vowels of the kri word (הַשֵּׁם or אֱלֹקִים), telling the reader which to say.
e.g. ה׳ — the four-letter name is written but read as הַשֵּׁם
Very common kri/ktivהִוא written for הִיא ("she") throughout Chumash — the vowels signal the kri
Jerusalemירושלם (ktiv, no yud) is read יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — the full spelling is only in the kri tradition
Leining ruleIn a Sefer Torah there are no vowels — the ba'al koreh must know the kri from memory; the Tikkun shows both
Maqqef מַקֵּף
A hyphen-like connector between two words — they are read as one unit with a single trop accent. The first word typically loses its accent.
e.g. כִּי־טוֹב — read as one rhythmic unit
לֹא־תִרְצַח — "you shall not murder" (sixth commandment) — לֹא loses its accent
וַיְהִי־כֵן — "and it was so" — very common phrase in Bereishit
Vowel change with maqqefShort words like כָּל, כֹּל, מָה often change their vowel when under maqqef: כׇּל־ (kamatz katan) before the connected word
Aleph vs Ayin
Both are "silent" in most modern pronunciations, but classically: א is a glottal stop, ע is a pharyngeal. For Torah reading, awareness of which is which affects accuracy — especially with vowels attached to them.
e.g. אָמַר vs עָמַד — both begin with "a" but different consonants
Aleph (glottal stop)אֶרֶץ, אֱלֹהִים, אָדָם — the aleph marks a distinct onset; in careful speech a slight catch in the throat
Ayin (pharyngeal)עַם, עֶבֶד, עוֹלָם — in Yemenite and some Sephardi traditions, clearly distinct from aleph
Why it matters for leiningConfusing aleph and ayin can change word meanings: אֵין (nothing) vs עַיִן (eye); אֹר (light) vs עוֹר (skin)
